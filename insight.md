# Dashboard Insights — Housing Price Analysis
### Northeast States, USA | Mr. Andreas' Budget: $2,000,000

![Dashboard](visualization\tableau_dashboard.png)
---

## 1. Price Distribution

The housing price distribution in Northeast States is heavily **right-skewed**, meaning most homes are concentrated at lower price points while a small number of luxury properties pull the average upward. The bulk of listings cluster between **$2.5M and $4.5M**, with the highest frequency of homes appearing around the **$3.5M mark**. Homes priced above $10M exist but are rare outliers that do not represent the typical market. This skew is important because it means the *average* price ($4.77M) is misleading — the *median* ($4.34M) is a more honest representation of what buyers typically encounter in this market.

---

## 2. Budget Feasibility

Mr. Andreas' budget of $2,000,000 places him at the very bottom edge of this market. Out of 545 total listings, only **9 homes (approximately 1.6%)** fall at or below the $2M mark. The remaining **536 homes (98.4%)** are priced above his budget. With a market median of $4.34M — more than double his available funds — the Northeast States housing market presents a significant financial challenge for Mr. Andreas as it currently stands.

---

## 3. Bedroom Count vs. Budget

Even the most modest homes in this dataset — those with just **1 bedroom** — carry an average price of around **$2.5M**, which already exceeds the $2M budget. As bedroom count increases, prices rise sharply: 3-bedroom homes average **$3.8M**, 4-bedroom homes average around **$5M**, and 5–6 bedroom homes reach **$6M or more**. Since Mr. Andreas needs at minimum 3 bedrooms to comfortably house his family of four, none of the standard family-sized homes fall within his current budget.

---

## 4. Area vs. Price Correlation

There is a clear and consistent **positive correlation between house area and price**. As the size of a home increases, so does its price — and this pattern holds true across all three furnishing categories (furnished, semi-furnished, and unfurnished). Homes that fall below the $2M threshold tend to have areas in the range of **1,500 to 3,500 square feet**. Furnished homes command a price premium at every size point compared to semi-furnished and unfurnished alternatives of the same area, confirming that furnishing status is a meaningful cost driver in this market.

---

## 5. Family Suitability

When applying both budget and bedroom filters simultaneously — homes priced at or below $2M **and** having at least 3 bedrooms — the results are stark. This combination accounts for **less than 1% of all listings** in the dataset. In practical terms, this means family-appropriate homes within Mr. Andreas' budget are nearly nonexistent in the current Northeast States market. The funnel chart makes this reality visually clear: the vast majority of listings fall into the "Above Budget" category, with only a thin sliver qualifying as both affordable and family-sized.

---

## 6. Furnishing Status & Price Heatmap

Across all bedroom counts, **furnished homes are consistently the most expensive**, followed by semi-furnished, then unfurnished. The heatmap reveals that the most affordable entry points in the entire dataset are **unfurnished 1-bedroom homes at approximately $2.275M** and **unfurnished 2-bedroom homes around $2.275M–$3.15M**. Semi-furnished homes offer a middle ground — lower than furnished but with some amenities already in place. For a budget-conscious buyer like Mr. Andreas, choosing unfurnished or semi-furnished over furnished can represent a meaningful reduction in purchase price, potentially bringing certain listings within or closer to his target range.

---

## 💡 Final Recommendation

The data tells a clear story: the Northeast States housing market is priced well above Mr. Andreas' current $2M budget. To find a home that genuinely suits his family's needs, he has two realistic paths. The first is to **increase his budget by 25–50%**, targeting the $2.5M–$3.0M range where 2–3 bedroom semi-furnished and unfurnished homes become accessible. The second is to **accept trade-offs** — specifically, targeting unfurnished homes in non-preferred areas without air conditioning, which tend to be priced 15–20% lower than comparable homes with those features. Either way, the most practical profile for Mr. Andreas would be a **2–3 bedroom, unfurnished or semi-furnished home in a non-preferred area, priced between $1.75M and $2.5M** — the sweet spot where affordability and family suitability come closest to intersecting.
